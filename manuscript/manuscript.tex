\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
All experiments are conducted using the MNIST dataset of handwritten digits. The input data consists of $28 \times 28$ grayscale images, which are flattened into vectors of size 784. Preprocessing involves a standard normalization transform with a mean of 0.1307 and a standard deviation of 0.3081. We utilize a consistent batch size of 1024 to maintain stable gradient estimates across all initialization regimes.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
To study the impact of heavy-tailed statistics on signal propagation, we employ a deep Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) with the following specifications:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Depth:} 9 hidden layers (10 layers total including the output).
    \item \textbf{Width:} A constant hidden size of 784 neurons per layer to maintain square weight matrices, facilitating Random Matrix Theory (RMT) analysis.
    \item \textbf{Activation:} The hyperbolic tangent ($\tanh$) function is used throughout the hidden layers.
    \item \textbf{Biases:} All bias terms are disabled ($b=0$) to isolate the dynamical effects of the weight matrix couplings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Heavy-Tailed Initialization Strategy}
The core of this research involves a parametric sweep over non-Gaussian weight initializations. Weights are sampled from a symmetric L\'{e}vy $\alpha$-stable distribution, $S_{\alpha}(\sigma)$. We explore a $3 \times 3$ grid of hyperparameters to compare different dynamical phases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stability Parameter ($\alpha$):} Fixed at $\{1.2, 1.5, 2.0\}$. Here, $\alpha=2.0$ serves as the Gaussian baseline, while $\alpha < 2.0$ introduces heavy-tails with infinite variance.
    \item \textbf{Scale Parameter ($g$):} Fixed at $\{0.25, 1.0, 3.0\}$. This parameter dictates the coupling strength $D_w^{1/\alpha}$, allowing us to move the network through ordered, critical, and chaotic regimes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Optimization and Training Protocol}
The networks are trained for 650 epochs using vanilla Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). We set a fixed learning rate of $\eta = 0.001$. Notably, we omit momentum and weight decay; this ensures that any observed structural evolution or "rich learning" behavior is a direct result of the interaction between the heavy-tailed initialization and the fundamental SGD trajectory. All simulations are executed on NVIDIA hardware using the PyTorch framework with a fixed seed ($s=0$) to ensure exact reproducibility across parameter sweeps.
\subsection{Time-Averaged Mean Square Displacement (TAMSD)}
To quantify the exploration of the parameter space by the network weights, we compute the Time-Averaged Mean Square Displacement (TAMSD). For a flattened weight vector $\mathbf{w}^l$ at layer $l$, the TAMSD at a lag time $\Delta t$ is defined by averaging over the total training time $T$:
\begin{equation}
    \overline{\delta^2}(\Delta t) = \frac{1}{T - \Delta t} \sum_{t=1}^{T - \Delta t} \| \mathbf{w}^l(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{w}^l(t) \|^2
\end{equation}
This approach provides a robust temporal average, smoothing out stochastic fluctuations inherent in individual SGD steps. We utilize a logarithmic sampling of $\Delta t \in \{1, 2, \dots, 128\}$ to capture dynamics across multiple time scales.

\subsection{The Diffusion Exponent ($\gamma$)}
The nature of weight propagation is characterized by the diffusion exponent $\gamma$. We extract this value by performing a linear regression on the log-log TAMSD plot:
\begin{equation}
    \log_{10}(\overline{\delta^2}) = \gamma \log_{10}(\Delta t) + C
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ determines the diffusion regime:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$\gamma \approx 1$:} Normal Brownian diffusion (typical of standard Gaussian learning).
    \item \textbf{$\gamma > 1$:} Super-diffusion (indicating anomalous transport facilitated by heavy-tailed weights).
    \item \textbf{$\gamma < 1$:} Sub-diffusion (suggesting the network is trapped in local minima or a "lazy" regime).
\end{itemize}
To ensure the accuracy of the fit and avoid initialization transients, we anchor the regression to the steady-state regime where $\Delta t \geq 4$.

\subsection{Cross-Layer Coupling and Correlation Matrices}
To investigate the structural redundancy and symmetry breaking mentioned in the primary research aims, we compute cross-layer correlation matrices. We calculate the dependency between the displacement vectors of layer $A$ and layer $B$ at a fixed lag ($\Delta t = 1$). Three metrics are employed to differentiate between linear and non-linear dependencies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pearson ($r$):} Measures the linear relationship between layer trajectories.
    \item \textbf{Spearman ($\rho$):} A rank-based correlation that identifies monotonic but potentially non-linear coupling.
    \item \textbf{Distance Correlation ($dCor$):} A robust metric capable of detecting arbitrary functional dependencies, where $dCor = 0$ implies true independence.
\end{enumerate}
These correlations are visualized as $10 \times 10$ heatmaps (representing the 9 hidden layers and 1 classification layer), allowing us to identify if heavy-tailed initializations lead to the "locking" of specific layer dynamics.
\subsection{Multi-Scale Weight Snapshots}
To investigate the structural evolution of the network throughout the training trajectory, we capture full model state-reflections at 45 non-linearly spaced intervals ($E \in \{0, 1, \dots, 625\}$). This snapshot strategy enables a detailed "fingerprinting" of every layer without the computational overhead of redundant training runs. We differentiate between \textit{Intrinsic Metrics}, which describe the current state of a weight matrix $W$, and \textit{Relative Metrics}, which measure the displacement from the initial state $W_0$.

\subsection{Intrinsic Spectral Fingerprinting}
For each weight matrix $W$, we compute a suite of metrics to characterize its spectral and distributional properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Empirical Tail Index ($\hat{\alpha}$):} We estimate the heavy-tailedness of the weight entries using a Hill Estimator. By selecting the top $k=1\%$ of absolute weight values, the estimator is defined as:
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{\alpha} = \left( \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \ln \frac{|w_{(i)}|}{|w_{(k)}|} \right)^{-1}
    \end{equation}
    where $w_{(i)}$ represents the $i$-th order statistic. This allows us to track whether the heavy-tailed nature $(\alpha < 2)$ of the initialization is preserved or diminished during training.
    \item \textbf{Spectral Gap:} Defined as the ratio of the two largest singular values $\sigma_1 / \sigma_2$. This serves as a proxy for identifying the emergence of discrete spectral outliers, a key prediction of heavy-tailed Random Matrix Theory.
    \item \textbf{Stable Rank:} A robust measure of the matrix's numerical rank, calculated as $\|W\|_F^2 / \|W\|_2^2$. This identifies the effective dimensionality of the features learned by each layer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Relative Evolutionary Metrics}
To quantify the "distance" traveled by each layer in the parameter space, we utilize relative measures compared to the initialization:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Relative Frobenius Norm:} Measures the total magnitude of weight change:
    \begin{equation}
        \|\Delta W\|_{rel} = \frac{\|W - W_0\|_F}{\|W_0\|_F}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Layer-wise Cosine Distance:} Tracks the rotational evolution of the weight vectors, identifying whether learning involves a simple scaling of initial features or a fundamental reorganization of the principal singular vectors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Layer-wise Dynamics and Ratio Analysis}
The evolution of these metrics is analyzed across the network's depth to identify the "learning burden" distribution. We specifically track the \textbf{Classifier-to-Hidden Ratio}, defined as the ratio of a metric in the final classification layer to the median value across all hidden layers. This ratio identifies phase transitions where the final layer converges faster than the internal feature extractors, a hallmark of the transition between lazy and rich learning regimes.
