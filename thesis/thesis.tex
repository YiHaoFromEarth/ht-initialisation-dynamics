\subsection{RMT as a Zero-Information Hypothesis}
The high levels of success deep neural networks have displayed in a variety of fields have led to much interest in understanding how they store the information required for a particular task. In particular, methods from Random Matrix Theory (RMT) are highly useful for this, serving as a null hypothesis. If the weight matrix of a neural network follows RMT predictions, it suggests that they are random and do not contain specific information.

However, not all methods from RMT are equally suitable for testing the zero-information hypothesis. For example, the distribution of singular values is highly sensitive to the specific type of random matrix. In contrast the nearest-neighbour spacing distribution and number variance are universal properties of random matrices, being invariant to dimension or initialisation. 

In principle, it is possible for information to be stored in weights that follow RMT predictions, for example a teacher-student setup with a random teacher. However, RMT analysis is particularly useful for image classification tasks on datasets like MNIST or CIFAR, where rules exist that can be encoded in a low-rank matrix.
